Hardware Description

5V Stepper Motor

Stepper motors are DC motors that move in discrete steps. They have multiple coils that are organized in groups called "phases". By energizing each phase in sequence, the motor will rotate, one step at a time.

With a computer controlled stepping you can achieve very precise positioning and/or speed control. For this reason, stepper motors are the motor of choice for many precision motion control applications.

Stepper Motor Driver (ULN2003)

ULN2003 IC is one of the most commonly used Motor driver IC. This IC comes in handy when we need to drive high current loads using digital logic circuits like Op-maps, Timers, Gates, PIC, ARM etc. For example a motor that requires 9V or 12 can be powered by external voltage source. This IC is commonly used to drive Relay modules, Motors, high current LEDs and even Stepper Motors.

First Prototype

At first, we decided to work with a stepper motor found in an old cd/dvd writer and design a H bridge. A H-bridge is a circuit configuration commonly used to control the speed and direction of a DC motor. The convenience of an H-bridge is that a low current digital signal can be used to control a high current motor. In the circuit diagram we see that the 4 mosfets surrounding the motor form an “H” shape. The mosfets are used as switches and are activated in diagonal pairs. To apply a forward voltage across the motor, mosfets 1=4=on and 2=3=off, causing the motor to spin in the forward direction (PWM=100% duty cycle). To make the motor spin in reverse, 1=4=off and 2=3=on

Final Model

After a couple of experiments, the first prototype went on fire so we needed to adopt a different approach.

The old stepper motor and H bridge were replaced by a new 5V Stepper Motor and a motor driver (ULN2003). The stepper motor is connected to the motor driver and the IN pins from the motor driver are connected to the C region of the ATMega. For our further work, we will use PORT C for coding.

Software Description

The software part mainly consists of 2 functions: clockwise and anticlockwise which are activated by a flag which is triggered by the SW1 button press. These functions are sequentially activating the pins in order to energise each coil.

In order for the motor to have more torque , we use both coils , a

technique called half stepping.  (using two “1” ’s instead of just one 1 per step . )

The motor will rotate clockwise, as a natural state. When we press the SW1 button it will rotate anticlockwise, then when SW2 is pressed the motor will turn clockwise again and so on. Basically the right switch is turning the motor to the right and the left switch is turning it to the left.
